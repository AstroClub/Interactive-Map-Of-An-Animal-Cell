Mitochondria

Functions:

Mitochondria mainly perform cellular respiration, also known as aerobic respiration. The 3 stages of cellular respiration as follows:

• Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm and involves converting a 6-carbon carbohydrate glucose into two 3-carbon complexes called pyruvate through a series of enzyme-catalysed reactions. This produces a small amount of ATP quickly without using oxygen, but is inefficient and produces lactic acid as a byproduct.

• To maximise the amount of ATP produced, pyruvate undergoes the Citric Acid Cycle, also called the Krebs Cycle. Pyruvate enters the mitochondria and is oxidised to form carbon dioxide, converting some ADP to ATP in the process while also reducing NAD+ and FAD+ to NADH and FADH2 respectively.

• Afterwards, Oxidative Phosphorylisation occurs where the NADH and FADH2 transfer the electrons from pyruvate to the Electron Transport Chain, where they are transferred to multiple proteins before being reduced from oxygen to water. ATP is synthesised by the energy released in the Electron Transport Chain, and this is where most ATP is produced.

Mitochondria are also involved for ion homeostasis and apoptosis.
